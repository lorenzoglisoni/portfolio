Teacheasy

Teacheasy is a mobile application aimed at creating a better working environment inside schools: teachers communicate better between themselves and with the school board. Managing daily activities becomes easier and more efficient.
It was prototyped for a university course in a five people team.

WHY WAS TEACHEASY BUILT

Problems related to communication between teachers were detected by the dean of a local school, but a clear understanding of their causes was lacking.

We were so requested to explore teachers impressions on their work relationships and analyse their perspective on internal processes.

After talking with users, we prototyped Teacheasy as a way for teachers to communicate better between themselves and with the school board, and a tool to help them in managing their daily work life.

MY ROLE

I worked as UX Designer in a team of five students. I conducted user research to uncover problems felt by users and stakeholders. After analysing insights, I prototyped and tested both low and high-fidelity versions of it.

RESEARCH

Understand the school environment

When we initially sat down with the school dean, she expressed concerns for a lack of communication between her and the teachers, and also amid colleagues. Through interviews and a workshop, we both analysed this problem and uncovered some hidden ones.

Four user interviews

I personally interviewed 4 teachers.

Interviews were planned to validate the issues reported by the school board, obtain a better view of internal school processes, and understand teachers’ perception of problems.  

After transcribing interviews, it was possible to produce a list of frequent issues and topics to investigate further.

Co-design workshop

We wanted to investigate the context further, but keep on running interviews did not seem the best way to go. It was difficult for teachers to find time to attend them: the research process was at risk of drifting over the following weeks, something we’d liked to avoid!

As a solution to this issue, we gathered 12 teachers in the same room at the same time for a participatory design workshop.  In this way, extended knowledge was gained in a limited time frame.

We dug deeper into the topics that emerged from interviews and investigated their day to day life, how activities are structured in the school and divided between class teams.

INSIGHTS

What did we learn

At the end of research phase, we gather all the raw material, analysed it and draw insights. Four areas emerged as most problematic.

· What’s happening today?

Teachers would like to know if there are important events or news about their classes,  the school and the broad institute they are part of.

They often miss important events: those information are generally shared on a blackboard, making hard to keep track of what is happening.


· Weekly meetings: importants but inefficients

For all the teachers, weekly meeting are extremely important.

However, they come with lot of flaws: there’s no clarity about the topics to be discussed, the same topic is discussed many times over different meetings, it’s hard to keep track of past discussions. Often they are not focused, lengthy, repetitive.

· Hard to communicate between colleagues

Teachers have difficulties on passing information to their colleagues. This is due to their tight schedules that do not allow informal communication to happen frequently.

· Technology: far from optimal

Devices provided by the school are outdated and slow. Teachers are so mainly using their personal devices for managing online-related school activities.

INSIGHTS

Defining opportunity areas

We decided to focus on solving problems that emerged more frequently as a result of research: their solution had high value for our users.

We choose to start tackling the issues related to weekly meetings and communication between colleagues.

We brainstormed over ways to solve the issues, and on how the ideas generated would be translated into features of a mobile application.

Why a mobile application?

All teachers had a smartphone, and they often consider the device as part of their workflow. Computer belonging to the school are outdated and slow.

The document produced after brainstorming that lists opportunity areas and solutions.

PROTOTYPE

Keep it simple!

We had a list of features that we could bring into the app prototype, it was now time to build the prototype!

As the project had a limited time length, we decided to approach the first phases of prototyping working low fidelity, in order to allow space for mistakes and avoid wasting weeks of design work if the solutions did not work out the way we planned.

We planned prototyping phase following three steps: we produced a diagram of the features of the app, then moved to sketches and after that we built a paper prototype.

TEST

Show it to users

Once a satisfying structure of the application was produced, we immediately went back to the school for a first round of tests. We tested the paper prototype with 5 users.

I planned, ran and analysed the results of the testing session

Why testing now?
‍
We decided to test the paper prototype so early to rapidly gain information about users’ degree of understanding of the information architecture. This allowed us to adjust the prototype before moving to an higher fidelity version.

Testing session

TEST

What did we learn after testing

· Privacy is a concern

Teachers asked often who could have access to the platform.


· Clear navigation

All the users navigated in the prototype without aids from the research team.

· Daily overview needed

All the users expressed the need of having a quick look over daily activities.

· Copy needs improvement

More than half teachers seemed confused by the copy used for some sections of the platform.

ITERATE

Creating a high fidelity protoype

After collecting the insights coming from tests, I sat down with another team member and started working on the high-fidelity prototype of Teacheasy. The suggestions emerged during the testing phase were implemented in the high-fidelity prototype.


Working in Adobe XD, we designed all the screens, following Material Design guidelines. Some basic interactions were prototyped so to enhance the fidelity level of the prototype for future testing.

Overview of final features

Remember the main problems of weekly meetings and communication? This is how we solved them in the final prototype.
Better communication

1. Teachers can create virtual post-its, and post them on a virtual board inside the application.

2. Post-its helps in keeping track of the daily events and communication inside the class.

3. Post-its are an information tool, don't require replies, in order to avoid the possible social-network pressure effect.

4. By vanishing at the end of each week, we make sure post-its are used in the day to day life for quick and informal communications.

Improved weekly meetings

1. Teachers find a dedicated space to manage weekly meetings.

2. An archive function is added so to keep track of topics discussed over the weeks.

3. An agenda of the meeting is automatically composed at the end of the week, based on the topics proposed by the teachers.

4. Statistics about frequency of topics serve both to teachers and to the school major.

5. Following the concerns about privacy, a lock icon was added to ensure privacy of the weekly meetings.

2020 redesign (extra)

I’ve taken some time to redesign some of the screens, in order to strengthen my UI design skills. The original prototype is from 2018, following design patterns and visual design choices that could result quite outdated.

Results

The prototype was presented back to the school, and positively received by the major and the school teachers. The teachers liked how their problems were translated into features of the prototype, and the major appreciated the knowledge gained.

However, as the final result was a prototype that will be hardly implemented, we also provided a series of physical, ready to use paper templates to improve weekly meetings. The templates reproduced the features of the application in a paper based version.

In this way, teachers had an immediately actionable tool to better manage weekly meetings.
