My process

I believe that a perfect one-size-fits-all process does not exist. Nor it needs to be linear. Internal and external constraints impact the creation of digital products and services.

I so adapt my process to the context I’m working on. I ensure that different methodologies are applied for a purpose (define why we are doing something), and help in understanding the people impacted by my work and the context they are living in.


Thinking

Just enough research

Research is the first phase of every project I contribute to. I believe that in order to create solutions respondent to people needs, we have the obligation to understand who those people are. However, over the years I’ve learned that research needs to adapt to the context it happens in, not the opposite.

I’ve been lucky enough to practice exploratory user research collaborating on projects for clients as Google and Spotify, and I also experienced the constraints of the life of a small startup.

I’m now equipped with a set of research tools that I’m ready to adapt to the context I work in. Interviews, diary studies, observations, workshops, surveys. My research process has now become more flexible.

Make sense of all this mess

After research, I organise insights to help in defining solutions.
When it comes to ideating solutions, the only criteria I always follow is the need of considering the big picture.

To do so, I borrow tools and techniques from the fields of Service Design and Lean Startup, two disciplines I’m strongly interested in. I try to learn fast if ideas generated are worth, using methodologies as customer journey mapping and lean validation methodologies: build, measure, learn.

Doing

Be unpolished

After ideation phase, It’s time to prototype. The context defines the level of fidelity of prototypes. I’ve worked with paper prototypes, concepts, interactive prototypes. All different, but all respondent to a specific context. My main tool is Figma and I’ve recently started using Webflow as a fast way to bring my designs to life.

Here, it’s important to define what the purpose of the prototype is. And just after that, build it. Is there a less expensive, faster way to build to achieve the same result? Go for it then!
